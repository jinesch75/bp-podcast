# Podcast Script — "The House of the Greater Region: Four Countries, One Home"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 15 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today's topic is perfect for that, because it's all about crossing borders. It's about something called the Greater Region — and its home, the House of the Greater Region.

**ANNA:** The Greater Region. Tom, a lot of people have heard the words, but aren't quite sure what it means. Let's start there. What is the Greater Region?

**TOM:** So, the Greater Region is a big cross-border area, right in the heart of Europe. Picture the map. It brings together five regions, in four countries, with three languages.

**ANNA:** Four countries! Which ones?

**TOM:** Luxembourg, in the middle. Then a piece of France — the Lorraine area, in the Grand Est region. Two parts of Germany — the Saarland and Rhineland-Palatinate. And parts of Belgium — Wallonia, the Wallonia-Brussels Federation, and the German-speaking Community.

**ANNA:** So Luxembourg sits right at the centre of all of it.

**TOM:** Right at the heart. And it's big. We're talking about more than sixty-five thousand square kilometres, and over eleven point eight million people living there.

**ANNA:** Eleven point eight million. That's a lot of people. And they cross these borders all the time, don't they?

**TOM:** All the time. And here's the number that really matters for Luxembourg — about two hundred and seventy thousand people cross a border every single day to go to work.

**ANNA:** Two hundred and seventy thousand, every day.

**TOM:** Every day. That's the highest number of cross-border commuters anywhere in Europe. And here's a striking detail — about half of those commuters live in France, and around three-quarters of all of them come to work in Luxembourg.

**ANNA:** So for Luxembourg, this isn't an abstract idea. The Greater Region is, quite literally, who comes to work here every morning.

**TOM:** Exactly. The nurse, the builder, the banker, the teacher — many of them cross a border to get here. The Greater Region is daily life.

**ANNA:** Okay. So that's the "what". Now tell me about the home — the House of the Greater Region.

**TOM:** Yes. So, all this cooperation between four countries needs a place to live. And that place is a real building — the House of the Greater Region. In French, the Maison de la Grande Région. In German, the Haus der Großregion.

**ANNA:** And where is it?

**TOM:** It's here in Luxembourg — in Esch-sur-Alzette, in the south. On the boulevard J.F. Kennedy. It opened in twenty fifteen.

**ANNA:** So it's quite young — about ten years old.

**TOM:** Young, yes. It was created as a place for meeting, exchange, and communication — a single roof for all the people working on cross-border cooperation.

**ANNA:** So instead of everyone scattered across four countries, they have one shared house.

**TOM:** That's the idea. Under that one roof, you find several teams. The main one is the Summit Secretariat of the Greater Region. There's also the secretariat of the Economic and Social Committee. There's the team that manages the European funding programme, called Interreg. There's a representation of one of the German regions, Rhineland-Palatinate. And there's the cultural association, the Espace Culturel.

**ANNA:** So it really is a house full of different neighbours, all working toward the same thing.

**TOM:** Beautifully put. Four countries, many teams, one home.

**ANNA:** Now Tom, you said "Summit". That sounds important. What is the Summit of the Greater Region?

**TOM:** The Summit is the top political table. It's where the leaders of all these regions sit down together to set the direction. So — the Prime Minister of Luxembourg, the Minister-Presidents of the German regions, of Wallonia, the leaders from the French side, and so on. They meet as the Summit.

**ANNA:** So the actual heads of government, around one table.

**TOM:** Around one table. They set the strategy for working together. And the leadership rotates — each region takes a turn chairing the Summit, for twenty-four months, two years at a time.

**ANNA:** So everyone gets their turn to lead. That feels fair.

**TOM:** It's very deliberate. Nobody dominates. It's a partnership of equals. And below the Summit, there's the day-to-day machinery — a committee of personal representatives, and the Secretariat in the House, which actually keeps the work moving between the big meetings.

**ANNA:** And the Secretariat — that's the team in the House in Esch.

**TOM:** Exactly. Created in twenty thirteen, it coordinates and supports the whole cooperation, and its working groups. It's a key point of contact, and a major source of information about the Greater Region.

**ANNA:** Are there other bodies too?

**TOM:** Two more worth knowing. There's an Interregional Parliamentary Council — that's the parliaments of the regions, giving advice and recommendations. And there's the Economic and Social Committee — which brings together employers and employees, the trade and professional organisations, from across the border. It's actually the only cross-border body of its kind in Europe.

**ANNA:** So it's not only governments. It's parliaments, and workers and employers too.

**TOM:** The whole of society, around the same table. That's what makes it special.

**ANNA:** Let me ask the question many listeners are thinking. This is all very nice — but what does it actually do for me, in my daily life?

**TOM:** Great question. And the answer is — a lot more than people realise. Let me give some concrete examples. Let's start with work.

**ANNA:** Yes — because so many people here cross a border for their job.

**TOM:** Right. The Greater Region works to make that easier — open borders, free movement of workers, and cross-border training programmes, so a young person can learn a trade on one side and work on the other. There's even a special framework agreement for cross-border vocational training.

**ANNA:** So your apprenticeship in one country can count in another.

**TOM:** That's the goal. And there's a labour-market observatory that studies the cross-border job market, so decisions are based on real data.

**ANNA:** Okay, that's work. What about getting around — mobility?

**TOM:** Mobility is a big priority, because if you cross a border every day, traffic and transport are your daily reality. The Greater Region supports projects that combine public transport with greener ways of travelling. And on its website, there are journey planners — tools that help you plan a trip across the borders, with real-time information.

**ANNA:** So one tool can plan a journey that crosses from one country to another.

**TOM:** Exactly — because your journey doesn't care about the border, and neither should the timetable.

**ANNA:** Now here's one I find amazing. Health care across borders. Tell me about that.

**TOM:** This one really can save lives. The regions have built partnerships between hospitals and emergency services. So in certain border areas, a person can get medical care on the other side of the border — sometimes without extra cost or insurance trouble.

**ANNA:** So the nearest hospital, even if it's in another country, can be your hospital.

**TOM:** In those areas, yes. If you have an accident, the closest ambulance comes — not the closest ambulance in your own country. When minutes matter, that's everything.

**ANNA:** That's such a human way to think about a border. Okay — what about education?

**TOM:** Education is a real jewel. There's the University of the Greater Region. It links six universities, in four countries, and lets students move between them.

**ANNA:** Six universities working as one network.

**TOM:** As one network. More than a hundred and thirty thousand students, with cross-border courses, double diplomas, joint degrees. So a young person can study in several countries and come out with qualifications recognised across the Region.

**ANNA:** And the languages?

**TOM:** That's the everyday magic of this place. Three languages — French, German, and Luxembourgish woven through it. Growing up here, or working here, you live in a multilingual world. And the Region invests in language learning and partnerships between schools, exactly so people can move and work across the borders.

**ANNA:** And there's culture too, I imagine.

**TOM:** A lot of culture. Remember, back in two thousand and seven, Luxembourg and the Greater Region were together the European Capital of Culture for a whole year. That left behind a cultural association — the Espace Culturel — which is actually one of the neighbours in the House.

**ANNA:** So the House isn't only about politics and paperwork — there's culture living inside it too.

**TOM:** Culture, tourism, shared heritage, UNESCO world-heritage sites all across the Region. It's a place with a deep common history — and a lot to discover.

**ANNA:** Let's go back in time for a second. How did all this start? It can't have happened overnight.

**TOM:** No, it grew slowly, over decades. The first seed was back in nineteen sixty-nine — a commission set up between Germany and France. Two years later, in seventy-one, Luxembourg joined. Then the German regions, the French side, the Belgian partners came in over the years.

**ANNA:** So it built up piece by piece.

**TOM:** Piece by piece. And a big milestone was nineteen ninety-five — the very first Summit of the leaders, held here in Luxembourg, in Mondorf-les-Bains. That's when it really became a proper partnership. And then, in twenty fifteen, it got its physical home — the House in Esch.

**ANNA:** From an idea between two countries, to four countries with a shared house. That's quite a journey.

**TOM:** It really is. And it's worth pausing on why it matters. This is a part of Europe that, in the past, saw war and hard borders. And today, those same borders are places where an ambulance crosses to save a life, where a student studies in three countries, where two hundred and seventy thousand people cross every day just to go to work — in peace.

**ANNA:** When you put it that way, it's genuinely moving. The border went from a wall to a doorway.

**TOM:** From a wall to a doorway. That's exactly the spirit of the Greater Region.

**ANNA:** Let's make it really concrete, Tom. Can we follow one person?

**TOM:** Of course. Let's imagine a young woman — let's call her Léa. She grows up in Lorraine, in France, just across the border.

**ANNA:** A very common story around here.

**TOM:** Very. So, Léa studies through the University of the Greater Region — some classes in France, some in Luxembourg, some in Germany. She picks up her languages along the way. Then she finds a job in Luxembourg, and becomes one of the daily commuters.

**ANNA:** One of the two hundred and seventy thousand.

**TOM:** Exactly. She uses a cross-border journey planner to sort out her commute. If she's ever ill near the border, she may be treated at the nearest hospital, even across the line. And on weekends, she enjoys the culture and the heritage all over the Region.

**ANNA:** And behind all of that, quietly, is the work coordinated from the House in Esch.

**TOM:** Quietly, behind the scenes — yes. Léa may never set foot in that building. But the agreements made there are what let her life flow so smoothly across the borders.

**ANNA:** I love that. The House works for people who've never even seen it. Okay Tom — let's bring it home. If I'm listening, what should I take away, and where do I go?

**TOM:** Three simple things. First — the Greater Region is real, and it's yours. If you live or work here, you're already part of it. Five regions, four countries, three languages — one shared space.

**ANNA:** One shared space.

**TOM:** Second — it touches your daily life. Work, training, transport, health, study, culture. If you cross a border, or work with people who do, this cooperation is quietly helping you.

**ANNA:** And third?

**TOM:** Third — if you want to learn more, there's one place to look. The official website — granderegion dot net. There you'll find the journey planners, the training tools, the information, and the contact for the House itself, in Esch-sur-Alzette.

**ANNA:** A short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. The Greater Region brings together five regions in four countries — Luxembourg, plus parts of France, Germany and Belgium — over eleven million people, and the biggest flow of cross-border workers in Europe. It's coordinated from the House of the Greater Region, in Esch-sur-Alzette, which opened in twenty fifteen and hosts the Summit Secretariat and several partners. Together they make daily life across the borders easier — in work, transport, health, study and culture. And you can find it all at granderegion.net.

**ANNA:** And the message for today?

**TOM:** That the border around Luxembourg isn't really an edge — it's a meeting point. Four countries chose to build a home together, instead of walls. And we all live a little better because of it.

**ANNA:** So... whether you're a resident or a cross-border worker — remember, you're part of something bigger. The Greater Region. And its home, the House of the Greater Region.

**TOM:** Four countries, one home, millions of lives connected. That's worth knowing about.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about the House of the Greater Region, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and looking out for each other. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!

*Sources: granderegion.net (official website of the Greater Region / Summit Secretariat). The Greater Region: 5 regions, 4 countries, 3 languages — Luxembourg, Lorraine (Grand Est, France), Saarland and Rhineland-Palatinate (Germany), Wallonia, the Wallonia-Brussels Federation and the German-speaking Community of Belgium; 65,401 km²; 11.8 million inhabitants; about 270,000 daily cross-border commuters (the most in Europe). Origins in a 1969 Franco-German commission; Luxembourg joined in 1971; first Summit in Mondorf-les-Bains in 1995; Summit Secretariat created in 2013 (EGTC/GECT under Luxembourg law). The House of the Greater Region (Maison de la Grande Région / Haus der Großregion), 11 boulevard J.F. Kennedy, Esch-sur-Alzette, opened in 2015 and hosts the EGTC Summit Secretariat, the Economic and Social Committee secretariat, the Interreg managing authority, the representation of Rhineland-Palatinate and the Espace Culturel. General information only — see granderegion.net for current details.*
