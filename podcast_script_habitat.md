# Podcast Script — "The Observatoire de l'Habitat – Understanding Luxembourg's Housing Market"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 15 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome back! It's a pleasure to have you with us today.
**TOM:** Hello everyone!
**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That is a programme about living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and cross-border workers too, to understand the country a little better.
**TOM:** And today's topic is one that almost everyone in Luxembourg talks about... housing.
**ANNA:** Where to live, how much it costs, whether to rent or buy... these are big questions, and big numbers.
**TOM:** But here is something many people don't know. Luxembourg has an official body whose job is to study all of this... carefully, and with real data.
**ANNA:** It is called the Observatoire de l'Habitat. In English, we could say the Housing Observatory.
**TOM:** I like that word, "observatory". It sounds like a place where people watch the stars.
**ANNA:** It's a nice image! But instead of watching the stars, this observatory watches the housing market.
**TOM:** So Anna, what exactly is the Observatoire de l'Habitat?
**ANNA:** It was created in 2003, inside the Ministry responsible for housing... today that is the Ministry of Housing and Spatial Planning.
**TOM:** And its purpose is to produce reliable information about housing in Luxembourg.
**ANNA:** Information that helps the government plan its housing policy, and that also informs the public... people like you and me.
**TOM:** So it has really two jobs. First, to support decision-makers, and second, to inform citizens.
**ANNA:** Exactly. Its mission is to collect information, to analyse it, and then to share it openly.
**TOM:** Collect, analyse, share. That's a good way to remember it.
**ANNA:** And it does not work completely alone. It has a key partner... a research institute called LISER.
**TOM:** LISER is the Luxembourg Institute of Socio-Economic Research. It is a public research centre.
**ANNA:** They bring scientific rigour... proper methods, careful analysis. So the numbers you get are solid, not guesses.
**TOM:** Good. So what does the Observatoire actually watch? What does it measure?
**ANNA:** Several things. First, the sale prices of homes... how much it costs to buy.
**TOM:** Second, rents... how much it costs to rent a home.
**ANNA:** Third, the price of land... building land, where new homes can go.
**TOM:** And it goes further than just prices, doesn't it?
**ANNA:** Yes. It also studies how much building land is actually available, and who owns it.
**TOM:** That's called land observation, or in French, the "observation foncière". They even do a kind of census of building land.
**ANNA:** It also follows how many new homes are being produced, year after year.
**TOM:** And there is a more human side too... the social and economic side.
**ANNA:** Right. The Observatoire studies housing affordability... whether people can actually afford to live where they need to.
**TOM:** And it looks at how public policies affect the cost of housing. So it's not just numbers, it's about real life.
**ANNA:** Let's talk about where these numbers come from, because that's important for trust.
**TOM:** Yes. If you give people figures, they want to know the figures are real.
**ANNA:** So, for sale prices, the data comes from notarial deeds. Every time a property is sold, a notary records it officially.
**TOM:** Those official records are passed each month to the Observatoire, by the Registration and Domains Administration.
**ANNA:** And they are completed with information from the Land Registry and Cadastre, for example the surface area of each apartment.
**TOM:** So these are not adverts or estimates. They are real, completed sales. That's about as reliable as it gets.
**ANNA:** For rents, it's a bit different. Rent data comes mostly from property listings... homes advertised for rent.
**TOM:** So rents reflect what is being asked on the market, while sale prices reflect deals that actually happened.
**ANNA:** There is one interesting limit, though. The Observatoire publishes detailed prices for apartments, but not for houses.
**TOM:** Why is that, Anna?
**ANNA:** Because for apartments, there is good data on the surface area, so you can calculate a fair price per square metre.
**TOM:** But for individual houses, that detailed surface information isn't available in the same way.
**ANNA:** So rather than publish numbers that could mislead people, they choose not to publish detailed house prices. That's honest.
**TOM:** I respect that. Better to say "we don't have reliable data" than to invent it.
**ANNA:** Now, let's give some real numbers, so this feels concrete.
**TOM:** Yes please. What does an apartment actually cost?
**ANNA:** In the third quarter of 2025, an existing apartment cost on average about 7,600 euros per square metre.
**TOM:** And a brand-new apartment, still under construction, was higher... around 10,000 euros per square metre.
**ANNA:** These are averages, of course. The price depends a lot on the commune, and on the quality of the home.
**TOM:** And the numbers move every quarter. They go up, they go down. That's exactly why the Observatoire keeps watching.
**ANNA:** For example, in that same period, the price of existing apartments had actually fallen a little compared with the quarter before.
**TOM:** So the market is not just one straight line going up. It breathes... it changes.
**ANNA:** And here is the part I love. All of this data is public, and you can explore it yourself.
**TOM:** The statistics, broken down by commune, are published on the national open-data platform, data dot public dot lu.
**ANNA:** So you can look up your own commune, and see how prices have moved there.
**TOM:** There is even a price simulator on the Observatoire's website, to help you get an idea.
**ANNA:** Imagine you are thinking of buying or renting in a certain town. You can check the typical prices before you start.
**TOM:** That's real power for ordinary people. Information that used to be hard to find is now open to everyone.
**ANNA:** The Observatoire also publishes reports and notes, written in plain language, on specific questions.
**TOM:** For example, there have been reports on how prices have changed over the very long term... going back to the 1970s.
**ANNA:** And reports on the rental market, and even on the regulation of rents... the rules that protect tenants.
**TOM:** So if a topic is in the news, there's often a serious, neutral study you can read about it.
**ANNA:** Let's come back to that word, affordability, because it matters so much here.
**TOM:** Affordability means... can people actually pay for their housing, without it taking over their whole budget?
**ANNA:** The Observatoire looks at the "effort rate"... the share of your income that goes to housing.
**TOM:** And it compares Luxembourg with neighbouring countries, to put things in context.
**ANNA:** This kind of analysis helps everyone see the real situation clearly, instead of just guessing or worrying.
**TOM:** And it helps the government design better policies, like support for tenants, or building more affordable homes.
**ANNA:** Let's not forget the land side, because in a small country, land is precious.
**TOM:** The Observatoire studies how much building land exists, and how much of it is actually being used.
**ANNA:** Sometimes land is available on paper, but it is not built on. Understanding that helps plan for the future.
**TOM:** So in a way, the Observatoire is like a health check for the whole housing system.
**ANNA:** That's a lovely way to put it. It measures the temperature, it spots the trends, and it shares the diagnosis with everyone.
**TOM:** So Anna, let's make this practical. How can a normal person actually use all this?
**ANNA:** First, before you rent or buy, check the typical prices in the commune you are interested in.
**TOM:** Second, if you want to understand a debate in the news, look for an Observatoire report on that topic.
**ANNA:** Third, remember that the numbers are official and neutral. They are not trying to sell you anything.
**TOM:** And fourth... knowledge reduces stress. Housing is emotional, but good information helps you make calmer decisions.
**ANNA:** Let's bring it all together. What should people remember about the Observatoire de l'Habitat?
**TOM:** First... it is Luxembourg's official housing observatory, created in 2003, within the Ministry of Housing.
**ANNA:** Second... it watches sale prices, rents, land, and how affordable housing really is, with help from the research institute LISER.
**TOM:** Third... its data is reliable, because sale prices come from real, notarised transactions.
**ANNA:** Fourth... the data is public. You can explore prices by commune on data dot public dot lu, and read clear reports.
**TOM:** And fifth... you can use it in your own life, to make better, calmer housing decisions.
**ANNA:** Housing can feel overwhelming. But you are not powerless. There is solid, honest information out there for you.
**TOM:** And knowing where to find it is half the battle.
**ANNA:** Thank you so much for listening today.
**TOM:** Take care, and see you in the next episode. Äddi!
**ANNA:** Äddi!
