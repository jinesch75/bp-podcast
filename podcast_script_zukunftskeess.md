# Podcast Script — "The Zukunftskeess: Money and Support for Your Children"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 15 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today's topic is one that brings a lot of joy, and also a lot of questions. It's about children — and the money and support the State gives to families.

**ANNA:** And there's one office at the centre of all this. Tom, tell us its name.

**TOM:** It's called the Zukunftskeess. In English we can say the Children's Future Fund. In French it's the Caisse pour l'avenir des enfants. People also use the short name — C-A-E.

**ANNA:** Zukunftskeess. That's a lovely name — the "future fund". Let's start simply. What is it?

**TOM:** The Zukunftskeess is the official State fund that pays family benefits in Luxembourg. So, the money for raising children. Its home on the internet is c-a-e dot l-u.

**ANNA:** cae.lu. And it's official — it's the State.

**TOM:** Completely official. It's part of the social security system. Not a private company. And it handles all the main benefits connected to having and raising children.

**ANNA:** Okay. So if I have a baby, or I have children already, this is the office that helps me.

**TOM:** Exactly. Let me give you the big picture first. There are really three main money benefits. The birth allowance, around the time the baby is born. The family allowance, every month as the child grows. And the back-to-school allowance, once a year for school.

**ANNA:** Birth, monthly, and back-to-school. Let's take them one at a time. Start with the birth.

**TOM:** So, the birth allowance — in French, the prime de naissance. It's a flat amount paid around the birth of a child. The total is about one thousand seven hundred and forty euros.

**ANNA:** That's a real help, right when a family needs it. And is it paid all at once?

**TOM:** No — and this is the clever part. It comes in three equal parts, of about five hundred and eighty euros each. And each part is linked to taking care of mother and baby.

**ANNA:** Tell me about the three parts.

**TOM:** The first is the prenatal allowance — before the birth. To get it, the expectant mother goes to her medical check-ups during pregnancy — examinations with the doctor, and also a dental check.

**ANNA:** So the first part rewards looking after yourself, and the baby, during pregnancy.

**TOM:** Exactly. The second part is the birth allowance itself — paid after the birth, with a check-up for the mother. And the third part is the postnatal allowance — paid after the child's medical check-ups in the first two years of life.

**ANNA:** Ah, I see the idea. The money is tied to health checks — for mum and for baby.

**TOM:** That's the whole philosophy. It's not only money — it's a gentle way to make sure mother and child see the doctor at the right times. And here's a nice detail — the three parts are independent. If you miss the conditions for one part, you can still receive the others.

**ANNA:** Good to know. And how long do you have to apply?

**TOM:** Generally within one year — for the first two parts, within a year of the birth. And for the postnatal part, within a year of the child's second birthday. The forms come from your doctor, the maternity hospital, or the commune — and you send them to the Zukunftskeess.

**ANNA:** Perfect. Okay — that's the birth. Now the big one, I think. The monthly family allowance.

**TOM:** Yes. This is the one most people know. In Luxembourgish people often just say "Kannergeld" — children's money. It's paid every single month, for each child.

**ANNA:** From when to when?

**TOM:** From the month the child is born, until they turn eighteen. And — this is important — it can continue up to the age of twenty-five if the young person is still in secondary school, or doing an apprenticeship.

**ANNA:** So as long as the child is still at school, the support keeps going. And how much is it?

**TOM:** It's set per child, per month. Right now it's a bit over three hundred euros per child each month. And here's a key point — it grows as the child gets older.

**ANNA:** It grows? How does that work?

**TOM:** There are two steps up. The amount increases when the child turns six. And it increases again when the child turns twelve. The idea is simple — older children cost more.

**ANNA:** That makes sense — teenagers are expensive! So a young child gets the base amount, a child over six gets a bit more, and a child over twelve gets more again.

**TOM:** Exactly. Now — one thing I should be honest about. These amounts are tied to the cost of living. They go up over time, with what's called the index. So for the exact figure today, you check the official page — it's the "social parameters" page on guichet dot l-u.

**ANNA:** Good. We give the idea, but the precise number — always check the official source. When is the money actually paid each month?

**TOM:** Every month, and at the latest by the twenty-fifth of the month. It lands in your bank account that day or the next. So it's regular, reliable money you can count on.

**ANNA:** And do I have to ask for it again every month?

**TOM:** No. You apply once, when the child is born or when you arrive in Luxembourg. After that it just comes automatically. You only need to tell the Zukunftskeess if something changes.

**ANNA:** Okay. So that's birth, and monthly. Now the third — back-to-school.

**TOM:** Yes — the back-to-school allowance. In French, the allocation de rentrée scolaire. It's an extra amount, once a year, to help with the cost of going back to school. Books, bags, supplies.

**ANNA:** Every parent knows that August feeling — buying everything for the new school year.

**TOM:** Exactly. And here's the good news — you don't have to apply for it separately. If your child already gets the family allowance, the back-to-school allowance comes automatically.

**ANNA:** Automatically. I love that. And when does it arrive?

**TOM:** In August, just before the school year. And the amount depends on the child's age. For children from six to eleven, it's around one hundred and fifteen euros. For children twelve and over, it's around two hundred and thirty-five euros.

**ANNA:** So again — older child, bigger amount. The same logic.

**TOM:** The same logic all the way through. Younger children, base amounts. Older children, more support.

**ANNA:** Tom, let me ask about a worry many families have. What about cross-border workers? People who work in Luxembourg but live in France, Belgium, or Germany?

**TOM:** Great question — and very important here, because so many people commute. The short answer is — yes, if you work in Luxembourg and pay into the Luxembourg social security system, your children can have the right to the family allowance, even if they live across the border.

**ANNA:** Even if the children live in another country?

**TOM:** Even then. This comes from European rules. Now, there's a detail — if you already receive a family benefit in your country of residence, Luxembourg doesn't pay twice. It pays the difference — what they call a differential supplement — so you receive the higher of the two.

**ANNA:** So you're not worse off. You get topped up to the Luxembourg level.

**TOM:** That's the idea. And it works for pensioners too — a retired cross-border worker with a Luxembourg pension keeps the right to the family allowance.

**ANNA:** That's reassuring for a lot of families. Now — let's talk about how you actually deal with the Zukunftskeess. Is it a lot of paperwork?

**TOM:** It's much easier than it used to be. Most things can be done online, through MyGuichet.lu — the State's digital platform. You can apply for the family allowance there, with a secure login like LuxTrust or the eID card.

**ANNA:** Ah, MyGuichet.lu — we've talked about that in another episode.

**TOM:** Exactly — it's the online door to the administration. And through it you can also send documents to the Zukunftskeess, change your bank details, or get a payment certificate. There's even an online assistant that guides you through the family allowance application, step by step.

**ANNA:** And if someone isn't comfortable online?

**TOM:** Then you can still do it on paper. The forms are on the website, and also at the commune. You fill them in and send them by post. And if you have questions, you can call the Zukunftskeess, or visit their offices — they're on the Boulevard Royal, in Luxembourg City.

**ANNA:** So there's always a way — online, by post, or in person. Now Tom, are these three benefits everything the Zukunftskeess does? Or is there more?

**TOM:** There's more. Let me mention two other important things it's involved in. First — for parents who take time off work to look after a young child, there's parental leave, and the Zukunftskeess pays the parental leave income during that time.

**ANNA:** So when a mum or dad pauses their job for the baby, they're not left with nothing.

**TOM:** Right — there's a replacement income while they're on parental leave. And second — there's the childcare voucher, the chèque-service accueil. It makes crèches, day care, and supervised activities cheaper for families.

**ANNA:** So that's help with the cost of childcare — which can be a huge expense.

**TOM:** A huge expense, yes. So the Zukunftskeess really sits at the centre of family life — from pregnancy, to the baby years, to school, to childcare, to time off work.

**ANNA:** And there's also extra support for children with special needs, isn't there?

**TOM:** There is. For a child with a disability, there's a special supplementary allowance on top of the normal family allowance. So families who need more help, get more help.

**ANNA:** That's a caring system. Tom, let's make it really concrete. Can we follow one family?

**TOM:** Of course. Let's imagine a couple — they're expecting their first baby.

**ANNA:** Exciting! So where does the Zukunftskeess come in, from the very start?

**TOM:** Even before the birth. During the pregnancy, the mother goes to her check-ups, and the dental visit — and that opens the first part of the birth allowance, the prenatal part. About five hundred and eighty euros.

**ANNA:** Then the baby arrives.

**TOM:** The baby arrives. They register the birth at the commune, and that triggers the second part of the birth allowance. And they make one application for the monthly family allowance — through MyGuichet.lu.

**ANNA:** And from then on, the monthly money starts.

**TOM:** Every month, by the twenty-fifth — a bit over three hundred euros, automatically. Then, over the first two years, they take the baby to its medical check-ups, and that unlocks the third part of the birth allowance.

**ANNA:** So in the first two years, they've received all three parts of the birth allowance, plus the monthly allowance.

**TOM:** Exactly. The child turns six — the monthly amount goes up a step, and the back-to-school allowance begins each August. The child turns twelve — another step up. And it all continues, right through school, possibly to age twenty-five.

**ANNA:** And the parents only really did one big thing — they applied once, and kept their details up to date.

**TOM:** That's the beauty of it. Apply once, keep your information current, and the support flows. Calm and predictable, through the whole childhood.

**ANNA:** I really like that picture. Okay Tom — let's bring it home. If I'm listening, and I have children, or one on the way — what should I do?

**TOM:** Three simple steps. First — go to the website, c-a-e dot l-u, or to MyGuichet.lu. That's where everything starts.

**ANNA:** cae.lu. Got it.

**TOM:** Second — if you're expecting, start the birth allowance early, by going to your check-ups, and apply for the monthly family allowance when the baby is born. You only do it once.

**ANNA:** And third?

**TOM:** Third — keep your details up to date, especially your bank account and your situation. And if your child turns eighteen but stays in school, send back the form to keep the allowance going up to twenty-five.

**ANNA:** And for cross-border workers?

**TOM:** Same office, same website. If you work in Luxembourg, your children may have the right too — so don't assume you're not eligible. Check with the Zukunftskeess.

**ANNA:** A short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. The Zukunftskeess — the Children's Future Fund, cae.lu — is the State office for family benefits in Luxembourg. Three main ones. A birth allowance of about one thousand seven hundred euros, in three parts, linked to health check-ups. A monthly family allowance for each child — a bit over three hundred euros — that grows at age six and age twelve, and can last until twenty-five if the child stays in school. And a back-to-school allowance each August, automatically. Plus parental leave income, childcare vouchers, and extra help for children with special needs.

**ANNA:** And the message for today?

**TOM:** If you have children in Luxembourg, this support is yours by right — and it's easier to get than people think. One office, one website, and a system that walks beside your family for years.

**ANNA:** So... whether you're a new parent, an old hand, or a cross-border worker — remember the name. The Zukunftskeess. cae.lu.

**TOM:** One fund, looking after the future of your children. That's exactly what the name says.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about the Zukunftskeess, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and looking out for each other. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!
