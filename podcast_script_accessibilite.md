# Podcast Script — "Accessibility for Everyone: Luxembourg's Three Promises"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 15 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today's topic is one that touches every single one of us, sooner or later. It's about accessibility.

**ANNA:** Accessibility. Okay, Tom — that's a big word. What do we actually mean by it?

**TOM:** Good question. Accessibility means making the world usable by everyone — including people with a disability. A person in a wheelchair, a person who can't see well, a person who can't hear well, an older person who's a bit slower than before.

**ANNA:** So it's about removing barriers.

**TOM:** Exactly. And here's the key idea — and it comes from the World Health Organization. Disability isn't only a health problem. It comes from the meeting between a person and the world around them. If the world is built badly, the person is disabled by it. If the world is built well, the barrier disappears.

**ANNA:** Ah, I like that. So the problem isn't always the person — it's often the building, or the website, or the machine.

**TOM:** Precisely. And that's why Luxembourg has built an official place online to bring it all together — a website called accessibilite dot l-u.

**ANNA:** accessibilite.lu. And who is behind it?

**TOM:** It's the State — the Government of Luxembourg. So it's official, public information. And the website is a kind of front door, that opens onto three big areas.

**ANNA:** Three areas. Okay, let's take them one by one. What's the first?

**TOM:** The first is digital accessibility. That means websites and mobile apps. Making them usable for people with a disability.

**ANNA:** So, the online world.

**TOM:** Yes. Think about it — today you do so much online. You check your taxes, you book an appointment, you read the news. Now imagine you're blind, and you use a screen reader — a programme that reads the page out loud. If the website is built badly, the screen reader gets lost, and you're stuck.

**ANNA:** And you can't do the simple thing everyone else does easily.

**TOM:** Right. So Luxembourg passed a law — it's from the 28th of May, 2019. And it says that public sector websites and apps — the State, the communes, public bodies — must be accessible.

**ANNA:** So the official, public websites have a duty to be usable by everyone.

**TOM:** They have a legal duty. And there's a simple way to remember what "accessible" means here. Four words. The content must be perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust.

**ANNA:** Let's unpack those, slowly.

**TOM:** Sure. Perceivable — you can perceive it. For example, every image has a text description, so a screen reader can describe it. Operable — you can operate it. For example, you can use the whole site with just a keyboard, not only a mouse.

**ANNA:** Because some people can't use a mouse.

**TOM:** Exactly. Understandable — the page behaves in a clear, predictable way, and helps you fix mistakes. And robust — it works with assistive technologies, today and in the future.

**ANNA:** Perceivable, operable, understandable, robust. That's a nice checklist. And how do we know if a website actually follows the rules?

**TOM:** Each public website must publish what's called an accessibility declaration. It's an honest statement that says — we are fully compliant, partly compliant, or not compliant. And it lists the problems that still exist.

**ANNA:** So it's transparent. They have to admit what's not perfect yet.

**TOM:** Exactly. And — this is important — if you find a problem, you can complain. Every site must give you a way to report a barrier. They have to answer you, normally within one month. And if you're not satisfied, you can turn to the State information service, or even the ombudsman — the médiateur.

**ANNA:** So you're not powerless. If a public website blocks you, there's a door to knock on.

**TOM:** Always. That's the whole point. Okay — shall we go to the second area?

**ANNA:** Yes please. We've done the digital world. What's next?

**TOM:** The second area is infrastructure. That means the physical world — buildings, public spaces, and streets.

**ANNA:** Ah, the concrete world. Ramps, doors, lifts.

**TOM:** Exactly. Can you enter the building? Is there a ramp as well as steps? Is the door wide enough for a wheelchair? Is there a lift? Are there things to help a blind person find their way?

**ANNA:** And is there a law for this too?

**TOM:** There is. It's the law of the 7th of January, 2022, and it came into force on the 1st of July, 2023. And it's built on a beautiful idea called universal design.

**ANNA:** Universal design. What does that mean?

**TOM:** It means designing a place, from the very start, so that everyone can use it. Not a special side entrance for some people, and the main entrance for the others. One place, that works for all.

**ANNA:** So instead of building a barrier and then fixing it later...

**TOM:** ...you build it right the first time. And here's the clever part — it's actually cheaper. The United Nations says it: if you design for everyone from the beginning, it costs much less than removing barriers from a building that's already finished.

**ANNA:** That makes complete sense. It's harder and more expensive to add a lift to an old building than to plan it from day one.

**TOM:** Exactly. And the new law goes further than the old one. It's not only about public buildings. It also covers public roads, and collective housing — buildings with many homes inside.

**ANNA:** And for buildings that already exist? You can't rebuild everything overnight.

**TOM:** No, and the law is realistic. New buildings and big renovations must follow the rules now. But for places that already exist and are open to the public, there's a deadline to become accessible — the 1st of January, 2032.

**ANNA:** So there's time, but there's also a clear target. Good. And who can help with all this — for someone building or renovating?

**TOM:** The infrastructure portal on accessibilite.lu has the rules, the procedures, and a toolbox. So an architect, a commune, or an owner can find what they need to do it right.

**ANNA:** Lovely. Okay, Tom — that's digital, and infrastructure. What's the third area?

**TOM:** The third one is the newest, and maybe the most surprising. It's the accessibility of products and services.

**ANNA:** Products and services. Now that sounds very broad. What does it include?

**TOM:** A lot of everyday things. Think about a bank machine — an ATM. A ticket machine at the station. An online shop. A banking app. An e-book. Smartphones and computers. These are the products and services we all use, all the time.

**ANNA:** And the idea is they should work for people with a disability too.

**TOM:** Exactly. This comes from a European law — people call it the European Accessibility Act. Luxembourg put it into national law with the law of the 8th of March, 2023. And it started to apply on the 28th of June, 2025.

**ANNA:** So this one is very recent — it's basically just begun.

**TOM:** Just begun. And it's a real change. For the first time, it's not only the State that has duties — it's private companies too.

**ANNA:** Ah, so businesses now have to think about accessibility.

**TOM:** Yes. If you make or sell certain products and services, they have to be accessible. Now — there's an exception for the very small businesses. Roughly speaking, the rules focus on companies with more than ten employees and a certain size of turnover. So the tiny shop on the corner isn't treated the same as a big bank.

**ANNA:** That seems fair. And who keeps an eye on all this?

**TOM:** There's a new office for exactly this. In short it's called OSAPS — the office that watches over the accessibility of products and services. It does two jobs. It checks that products and services on the market follow the rules. And it helps businesses — with guides, tools, and advice — to get it right.

**ANNA:** So it's not only a police officer. It's also a helper.

**TOM:** Both. Carrot and stick. And here's the part for us, the citizens — if you find a product or a service that isn't accessible, you can report it.

**ANNA:** How do I do that?

**TOM:** Online, through MyGuichet.lu — the State's digital platform. And you can even do it without logging in, if you prefer. So it's easy to raise your hand and say — this machine, this service, doesn't work for me.

**ANNA:** I love that the three areas connect like that. You report a faulty product through a public website... which itself has to be accessible... in a building that should also be accessible.

**TOM:** Exactly. It's one big circle. Digital, infrastructure, products and services. Three promises, one goal.

**ANNA:** Let's make it really concrete, Tom. Can we follow one person through a normal day?

**TOM:** Of course. Let's imagine someone — let's call her Maria. Maria has low vision. She doesn't see well.

**ANNA:** Okay. Maria wakes up. What's her day like, in a fully accessible Luxembourg?

**TOM:** She picks up her phone to check an official document. The public website is built well — so her screen reader reads it out clearly. That's the digital part.

**ANNA:** Good. Then she goes out.

**TOM:** She walks to the commune office. The street has guidance she can feel under her feet, the building has a clear entrance, and a lift inside. That's the infrastructure part.

**ANNA:** And then?

**TOM:** On the way home she stops at a ticket machine. It speaks the instructions out loud, and the buttons are easy to feel. That's the products and services part. Maria gets through her whole day — on her own, with dignity.

**ANNA:** Without needing to ask a stranger for help at every step.

**TOM:** That's the dream. Independence and dignity. And that's what these three laws are slowly building.

**ANNA:** And honestly — this helps far more people than we think. Not only people with a permanent disability.

**TOM:** So true. Think of a parent pushing a pram — they love a ramp too. Think of someone with a heavy suitcase, or a broken arm. Think of an older person whose eyes and ears are getting tired. Accessibility helps everyone, at some point in life.

**ANNA:** It's the classic idea — design for the people who need it most, and you make life better for absolutely everyone.

**TOM:** Exactly. A curb ramp was made for wheelchairs — but the whole town uses it.

**ANNA:** Okay, Tom — let's bring it home. If I'm listening, what should I remember, and what can I do?

**TOM:** Three simple things. First — remember the website. accessibilite dot l-u. It's the official front door to all three areas.

**ANNA:** accessibilite.lu. Got it.

**TOM:** Second — if a public website blocks you, you have the right to complain, and to get an answer. Don't just give up — report it.

**ANNA:** And third?

**TOM:** Third — if a product or a service isn't accessible, you can report that too, through MyGuichet.lu. Your voice helps make things better, for you and for the next person.

**ANNA:** And if you're a professional — an architect, a business owner, a website manager?

**TOM:** Then the website is for you in a different way. It has the laws, the standards, the toolboxes — everything you need to do your part. Because accessibility isn't a favour. It's becoming the normal way to do things.

**ANNA:** A short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. Luxembourg has one official hub — accessibilite.lu — for three areas of accessibility. Digital, with the law of 2019, for public websites and apps. Infrastructure, with the law of 2022, for accessible buildings, streets, and housing, built on universal design, with a deadline of 2032 for existing public places. And products and services, the newest, from the European Accessibility Act, applying since June 2025, watched over by the OSAPS office — with private companies now involved too.

**ANNA:** And the message for today?

**TOM:** Accessibility isn't only for "other people". It's for all of us — today, or tomorrow. A world built for everyone is simply a better world to live in.

**ANNA:** So... whether you're a citizen who hits a barrier, or a professional who builds things — remember the front door. accessibilite.lu.

**TOM:** One website, three promises, and a country slowly opening every door.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about accessibility, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and looking out for each other. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!
