# Podcast Script — "The National Social Inclusion Office: More Than a Cheque"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 14 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today we're talking about an office with a very gentle, very human job. It's called the National Social Inclusion Office.

**ANNA:** The National Social Inclusion Office. In French, the Office national d'inclusion sociale. People use the short name — O-N-I-S.

**TOM:** ONIS. That's it.

**ANNA:** Now Tom, in an earlier episode we talked about the National Solidarity Fund, and a benefit called REVIS — the social inclusion income. Is this connected?

**TOM:** It's directly connected — and this is the key to understanding ONIS. Do you remember, REVIS is Luxembourg's guaranteed minimum income. If your household income is too low, REVIS tops you up to a basic level.

**ANNA:** Yes — the floor that the State doesn't let you fall below.

**TOM:** Right. But here's the thing. The full name isn't just "minimum income". It's the "social inclusion income". And that word — inclusion — is the whole point. REVIS has two sides.

**ANNA:** Two sides. Tell me.

**TOM:** One side is the money — the cheque every month. That part is paid by the National Solidarity Fund, the FNS. But the second side is just as important. It's helping the person find their way back — back toward work, toward activity, toward being part of society again.

**ANNA:** And that second side — the inclusion side — is the job of ONIS.

**TOM:** Exactly. The FNS handles the money. ONIS handles the people, and their path forward. REVIS isn't meant to be a cheque you receive forever, alone at home. It's meant to be a bridge.

**ANNA:** I love that distinction. So ONIS is the office that walks beside you on the bridge.

**TOM:** Beautifully put. That's exactly what it does.

**ANNA:** Let's get the basics. What kind of office is ONIS, officially?

**TOM:** It's a government administration, under the Ministry of Family. It was created by the big REVIS reform — the law of twenty eighteen — and it started its work at the beginning of twenty nineteen. It actually replaced an older service that did similar work before.

**ANNA:** So it's relatively young, but built on older foundations.

**TOM:** Yes. And its mission, in plain words, is to help the people who are furthest from the job market take steps back toward it — at their own pace.

**ANNA:** Furthest from the job market. Who do you mean by that?

**TOM:** People who, for all kinds of reasons, can't simply walk into a job tomorrow. Maybe someone has been out of work for a very long time. Maybe there's a health problem, or a difficult family situation, or housing troubles, or a loss of confidence. Life can knock people down in many ways.

**ANNA:** So it's not just "go and find a job". The obstacles can be much deeper.

**TOM:** Much deeper. And that's the wisdom of how ONIS works. It doesn't treat everyone the same. It recognises that before some people can look for work, they first need to stabilise their life.

**ANNA:** Stabilise. That's an interesting word. Can you unpack it?

**TOM:** Of course. ONIS works with two kinds of measures. The first kind are called stabilisation measures. These are for someone who isn't ready for a job yet — because there are other things to sort out first. Health, personal difficulties, getting daily life back on track.

**ANNA:** So first, help me stand up steadily.

**TOM:** First, help you stand. And then, the second kind — activation measures. These are the steps toward work and activity. Building skills, gaining experience, getting back into a rhythm.

**ANNA:** So stabilisation first, then activation. It meets people where they actually are.

**TOM:** Exactly. It's not one-size-fits-all. It's a path shaped around each individual person, and how they're developing.

**ANNA:** Okay, let's make it practical. If I'm a person who starts receiving REVIS, what actually happens with ONIS? Walk me through it.

**TOM:** Sure. It usually begins with an invitation — you're invited to a meeting, a reception, with ONIS. And there, you sign something called a collaboration declaration.

**ANNA:** A collaboration declaration. That sounds formal. What does it mean, really?

**TOM:** In simple terms, it's a mutual promise. You agree to take part — to work together on your situation. And the State agrees to support you. It's a two-way street. You're not just receiving; you're participating.

**ANNA:** So it's a partnership, written down.

**TOM:** A partnership. And then comes the part I find really thoughtful. You're paired with a person — a regional social inclusion agent. In French, an "agent régional d'inclusion sociale", an ARIS.

**ANNA:** A regional agent. So a real human being, assigned to help you.

**TOM:** A real human being, who becomes your guide. And "regional" matters — these agents work inside the local social offices, around the country. So you're not sent to one big building in the capital. You're helped close to where you live.

**ANNA:** That's important. Help that's local, and personal.

**TOM:** Very. And together, you and your agent build something called an activation plan. It's a personal plan — your situation, your difficulties, your goals, and the concrete steps to move forward.

**ANNA:** So the plan is built with you, not handed to you.

**TOM:** With you. That's the heart of it. It's your plan. The agent brings the knowledge and the options; you bring your life and your hopes. Together, you map the road.

**ANNA:** And what kind of steps might be in that plan? Give me some examples.

**TOM:** It depends entirely on the person. For someone who needs stabilising first, it might be about health, or sorting out practical problems, or rebuilding a daily structure. For someone closer to work, it might be training, or gaining experience.

**ANNA:** And the work experience — how does that happen if you don't have a job yet?

**TOM:** One of the tools is a temporary placement in a useful, community role — often in a non-profit organisation. You contribute, you're active, you build experience and confidence — and that can become a stepping stone toward a real job.

**ANNA:** So you're doing something meaningful, and at the same time preparing yourself.

**TOM:** Exactly. It's dignity and preparation in one. You're not sitting at home feeling useless. You're contributing, and growing.

**ANNA:** Tom, I want to ask the honest question some listeners will have. Is this about helping people — or is it about checking up on them, making sure they're not being lazy?

**TOM:** That's a fair question, and an important one. The honest answer is — the spirit of it is help, not punishment. The whole design is to accompany people, at their own pace, around their own obstacles. It's true that REVIS asks for participation — that's the collaboration declaration. But the aim isn't to catch people out. The aim is to not abandon them.

**ANNA:** To not abandon them. Because just sending money, and nothing else...

**TOM:** ...can leave a person stuck and alone for years. And that's the trap REVIS and ONIS are trying to avoid. Money keeps you afloat. But inclusion — a guide, a plan, a path — that's what helps you move.

**ANNA:** That really reframes it. The cheque is the floor; ONIS is the staircase.

**TOM:** The cheque is the floor; ONIS is the staircase. I'll borrow that.

**ANNA:** Let's make it concrete with a person. Can we?

**TOM:** Let's. Imagine a man — let's call him Paulo. He's been out of work for several years. He lost his job, then his confidence, and slowly he drifted far from the labour market.

**ANNA:** A story that's more common than people think.

**TOM:** Far more common. So Paulo applies for REVIS. The FNS sorts out the money side — he gets the minimum income, so he can live. And then ONIS enters his life.

**ANNA:** He's invited to a meeting.

**TOM:** He's invited, he signs the collaboration declaration, and he's paired with a regional agent near his home. They sit down together. And at first, it's clear Paulo isn't ready to job-hunt — he's exhausted, a bit lost. So the plan starts with stabilisation.

**ANNA:** Help him stand up first.

**TOM:** Help him stand. Then, step by step, as he stabilises, the plan moves toward activation. Maybe a placement in a community organisation, to get back into a rhythm and rebuild his confidence. Then some training.

**ANNA:** And one day, perhaps, a real job.

**TOM:** That's the goal. And even if it takes time, Paulo is no longer alone with it. He has a plan, and a person walking beside him. That changes everything.

**ANNA:** It really does. The difference between drifting, and being accompanied.

**TOM:** That's the whole difference. And it's what "inclusion" really means — not leaving anyone outside.

**ANNA:** Let's zoom out for a second. Why does this matter for society as a whole, not just for the individual?

**TOM:** Because when someone moves from being stuck, to being active and included, everybody gains. The person regains dignity and income. Their family is more stable. And society keeps a member connected, contributing, instead of lost. Inclusion is good for the person, and good for all of us.

**ANNA:** A society is stronger when nobody is written off.

**TOM:** When nobody is written off. That's the quiet philosophy behind ONIS.

**ANNA:** Okay Tom, let's bring it home. If I'm listening, and this touches me, or someone I know — what should I take away?

**TOM:** A few simple things. First — if you receive REVIS, ONIS is not your enemy. It's the part of REVIS that's there to help you move forward, with a real person and a personal plan.

**ANNA:** The inclusion side, not just the money side.

**TOM:** Second — it works at your pace. If you need to stabilise first, that's allowed, that's normal. Stabilisation comes before activation. Nobody is pushed faster than they can go.

**ANNA:** And third?

**TOM:** Third — you're not alone. You get a regional agent, close to home, who builds the plan with you. If you ever feel lost in the system, that agent is your point of contact.

**ANNA:** And where can people learn more?

**TOM:** The official website is onis — that's o-n-i-s — dot gouvernement dot l-u. And for the REVIS benefit itself, the information is on guichet dot l-u, and the money side is the National Solidarity Fund, which we covered in another episode.

**ANNA:** A short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. ONIS, the National Social Inclusion Office, is the government office that handles the "inclusion" side of REVIS, Luxembourg's social inclusion income. While the National Solidarity Fund pays the money, ONIS helps the people furthest from work move forward — through stabilisation measures first, then activation measures. You sign a collaboration declaration, you're paired with a regional social inclusion agent near your home, and together you build a personal activation plan. The spirit is to accompany, not to abandon.

**ANNA:** And the message for today?

**TOM:** That a minimum income is only half of solidarity. The other half is inclusion — a hand, a plan, and a path back. Money keeps you standing; inclusion helps you walk.

**ANNA:** So... whether it's for you, or for someone you care about — remember, behind the REVIS cheque there's also a helping hand. The National Social Inclusion Office. ONIS.

**TOM:** Half the help is the money. The other half is not being left alone. That's worth knowing.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about the National Social Inclusion Office, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and looking out for each other. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!

*Sources: onis.gouvernement.lu (Office national d'inclusion sociale — National Social Inclusion Office). ONIS is a government administration under the Ministry of Family, created by the amended law of 28 July 2018 on the social inclusion income (REVIS); it began work on 1 January 2019, replacing the former National Social Action Service (SNAS). Its mission (Chapter 3 of the REVIS law) is the stabilisation and/or activation of people furthest from the labour market, through measures tailored to each person's needs and development; to coordinate the responsible authorities and organisations; and to collect statistical data on beneficiaries. ONIS centralises the reception of persons who must sign a collaboration declaration (article 13(1) of the REVIS law); beneficiaries are directed to a regional social inclusion agent (ARIS), working within the social offices, with whom they develop an activation plan. The financial part of REVIS is paid by the National Solidarity Fund (FNS). Office: 13C, rue de Bitbourg, L-1273 Luxembourg. General information only — see onis.gouvernement.lu and guichet.lu for current details and conditions.*
