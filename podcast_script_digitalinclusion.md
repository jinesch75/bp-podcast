# Podcast Script — "Digital Inclusion: A Computer, and the Skills to Use It"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 15 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today's topic is one I really love, because it's about fairness in the modern world. It's about a non-profit called Digital Inclusion.

**ANNA:** Digital Inclusion. Tom, let's start with the problem first. Why do we even need something like this?

**TOM:** Think about how much of life now happens through a screen. You apply for a job online. You book a doctor. You do your banking. You fill in official forms. You help your kids with school. Almost all of it needs a computer, and the skills to use one.

**ANNA:** And if you don't have a computer, or you've never learned how to use one...

**TOM:** ...then you're locked out. Not just a little inconvenienced — really locked out, of work, of services, of everyday life. People call this the "digital divide". On one side, people who are connected. On the other, people who are left behind.

**ANNA:** And that gap can be about money, or age, or just never having had the chance to learn.

**TOM:** Exactly. And that's the gap Digital Inclusion exists to close. It's a non-profit association — an "asbl" — here in Luxembourg. And its whole mission is to help everybody in the country get access to information technology.

**ANNA:** So who do they help, and how? Give us the big picture.

**TOM:** The big picture is beautifully simple. They do three things. First — they give people a computer. Second — they teach people how to use it. And third — they do it all in a way that's good for the planet.

**ANNA:** A computer, the skills, and good for the planet. I like that. Let's take them one at a time. Start with the computer.

**TOM:** So, Digital Inclusion gives away free second-hand computers to people who need them, here in Luxembourg.

**ANNA:** Free computers. Where do they come from?

**TOM:** They're donated. Companies and individuals give away their old laptops, desktops, smartphones and tablets. Then a team of volunteers cleans them up, repairs them, wipes the old data, and installs fresh software. A tired old laptop becomes a good, working machine again.

**ANNA:** So a computer that one person no longer wants becomes a lifeline for someone else.

**TOM:** Precisely. And the numbers are remarkable. Since they started back in twenty sixteen, they've handed out more than ten thousand computers. And over a thousand smartphones.

**ANNA:** Ten thousand computers! That's ten thousand people, or families, brought online.

**TOM:** And it keeps growing. In a single recent year, volunteers refurbished over two thousand machines. This year, twenty twenty-six, the organisation is even celebrating its tenth anniversary.

**ANNA:** Ten years of this work. Now, the practical question everyone's thinking — who can actually get one of these computers?

**TOM:** Good — let's be clear and concrete. To apply, first, you need to live in Luxembourg. And then, you need to meet one of a few conditions.

**ANNA:** What are they?

**TOM:** The main ones are these. Your household receives the cost-of-living allowance — the allocation de vie chère. Or you're a refugee — an asylum seeker, a person under temporary protection, or an unaccompanied minor. Or you hold a residence permit of the "private life" type. Or you're going through an official debt-settlement procedure for over-indebtedness.

**ANNA:** So it's really aimed at people on a low income, and at refugees and newcomers.

**TOM:** Exactly — the people for whom buying a computer would be a real struggle. And here's some good recent news. In the past, it was one computer per household. But since late twenty twenty-five, because they've received so many donations, each member of the household aged twelve or older can get their own computer.

**ANNA:** So now the teenager doing homework, and the parent looking for work, can each have their own machine.

**TOM:** Each their own. Adults apply for themselves, and an adult can apply for the children, aged twelve to eighteen, in their home. And the waiting time, right now, is usually under a month.

**ANNA:** Under a month — that's fast. And what kind of computer do you actually get? Some old, broken thing?

**TOM:** No, that's the lovely part. They come ready to use, with the operating system already installed — Windows, or a Mac, or sometimes Linux. A decent processor. And they even come with a free repair warranty — apart from the battery, which on an old laptop might be weak or missing.

**ANNA:** A free computer, that works, with a warranty. That's genuinely generous. Okay — that's the computer. Now the second part. The skills.

**TOM:** Yes — because a computer is no use if you don't know how to use it. So Digital Inclusion also runs classes. Free IT classes, to teach people the basics and beyond.

**ANNA:** And in which languages? Because Luxembourg is so multilingual.

**TOM:** In many languages. That's deliberate — they teach in several languages so that newcomers can learn in a language they understand. They even have a Language Lab, including one that uses AI, to help people practise.

**ANNA:** So you can learn computer skills and language skills at the same time.

**TOM:** Both at once. And there's something else I really like — the Open Classroom. Every Friday morning, you can just walk in, no appointment needed, and get help with a digital problem.

**ANNA:** Just walk in? So if I'm stuck — I can't log in somewhere, I don't understand an email, a form won't work...

**TOM:** ...you bring it in on a Friday morning, and someone sits with you and helps. No appointment, no charge. It's open from half past nine to half past one.

**ANNA:** That's wonderful. So many people just need a patient person to sit beside them for ten minutes.

**TOM:** Exactly. And it's not only individuals. They also support social organisations, and they offer device repairs — those by appointment, by email.

**ANNA:** Okay. The computer, the skills... now the third part. You said good for the planet. Tell me about that.

**TOM:** This is the part that makes the whole thing so clever. Think about it — every computer they give away is a computer that was about to be thrown out. Electronic waste is a huge environmental problem.

**ANNA:** All those old laptops in drawers, or in the bin.

**TOM:** Right. By collecting them, repairing them, and giving them a second life — and even a third life — Digital Inclusion keeps them out of the rubbish. So one single act helps a person AND helps the environment.

**ANNA:** It's a circle. The waste of one becomes the tool of another.

**TOM:** A beautiful circle. Social good and environmental good, in the same gesture. And here's a detail I find very moving — many of the volunteers who repair the computers are refugees themselves.

**ANNA:** Oh — tell me more about that.

**TOM:** So while they repair the machines, these volunteers get training, they build IT skills, and they meet people from the local technology world. They're learning, they're contributing, and they're connecting — all at once.

**ANNA:** So the workshop isn't only fixing computers. It's helping people find their feet in a new country.

**TOM:** Exactly. It's repairing machines and building lives at the same time. That's why this is a story about inclusion, not just technology.

**ANNA:** That really is special. Tom, let's make it concrete. Can we follow one person?

**TOM:** Of course. Let's imagine a woman — let's call her Amina. She's recently arrived in Luxembourg as a refugee. She has two teenage children. She has almost nothing, and no computer.

**ANNA:** A very real situation. How does Digital Inclusion change her story?

**TOM:** Step by step. First, because she's a refugee living in Luxembourg, she's eligible. She applies — and within about a month, she receives a working laptop. And because of the new rule, her two teenagers can get computers for their schoolwork too.

**ANNA:** So suddenly the whole family is online.

**TOM:** The whole family. Then, Amina doesn't feel confident using it. So she goes to a free class, in a language she understands, and learns the basics. When she gets stuck one week, she drops into the Open Classroom on Friday, and someone helps her set up her email and an official account.

**ANNA:** And now she can do the things the rest of us take for granted.

**TOM:** Now she can apply for jobs, fill in her forms, message her children's school, do her banking — on her own. That's not just a computer. That's dignity, and independence.

**ANNA:** And maybe, one day, she volunteers in the workshop herself.

**TOM:** Maybe she does. And then the circle is complete — the person who was helped becomes the person who helps. That happens there, all the time.

**ANNA:** I love that. Now, Tom, let's turn it around. Many of our listeners already have what they need. How can they help?

**TOM:** So many ways, and this is important. The simplest — if you have an old laptop, smartphone or tablet sitting in a drawer, doing nothing — donate it. Don't let it rot. Give it a second life.

**ANNA:** So that old laptop in the cupboard could become someone's lifeline.

**TOM:** Literally. Just wipe your worries — they handle the data cleaning securely. You can also give money, to help cover the costs. And you can volunteer — in the repair workshop, or helping to teach.

**ANNA:** And companies? They must have lots of old equipment when they upgrade.

**TOM:** Companies are a big one. When a business renews its computers, instead of scrapping the old ones, they can give them to Digital Inclusion. It's good for the community, and good for the planet — and a lovely thing for a company to be part of.

**ANNA:** Where do people find all this? The address, the forms, the classes?

**TOM:** Everything is on their website — digital dash inclusion dot l-u. That's digital, then a dash, then inclusion, dot l-u. You'll find how to apply, how to donate, the class schedule, and the contact details.

**ANNA:** And they have a real place you can visit?

**TOM:** They do. Since twenty twenty-four, they're in Bonnevoie, in Luxembourg City, near the main train station — the Gare. They're open every weekday, from half past nine in the morning to half past five in the afternoon.

**ANNA:** So it's a real, open door, not just a website.

**TOM:** A real, open door. With real people who want to help.

**ANNA:** Let's bring it home, Tom. If I'm listening, what should I take away?

**TOM:** Three things. First — if you, or someone you know, needs a computer and can't afford one, and you live in Luxembourg on a low income or as a refugee — help exists. A free, working computer, usually within a month.

**ANNA:** A free computer.

**TOM:** Second — if you have the computer but not the confidence, there are free classes, and a walk-in Open Classroom every Friday. You're never too old, or too new, to learn.

**ANNA:** And third?

**TOM:** Third — if you're one of the lucky ones who has everything you need, you can be part of this. Donate that old device. Give a little money. Or give your time. Turn your spare technology into someone else's opportunity.

**ANNA:** A short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. Digital Inclusion is a Luxembourg non-profit that closes the digital divide in three ways. It gives free, refurbished computers to people on low incomes and to refugees — more than ten thousand so far. It teaches digital skills, with free classes in many languages and a walk-in Open Classroom every Friday. And it protects the environment, by giving old devices a second life. Everything is at digital-inclusion.lu, and their door in Bonnevoie is open every weekday.

**ANNA:** And the message for today?

**TOM:** That in a world that runs on screens, no one should be left behind for the lack of a computer or a little help to use it. A donated laptop, a patient teacher, a welcoming workshop — small things that change lives.

**ANNA:** So... whether you need help, or you'd like to give it — remember the name. Digital Inclusion. digital-inclusion.lu.

**TOM:** A computer, and the skills to use it. That's a door into the modern world — open to everyone.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about Digital Inclusion, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and looking out for each other. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!

*Sources: digital-inclusion.lu (Digital Inclusion a.s.b.l.), including the "Our goals" and "How to apply for a free second-hand computer" pages. Digital Inclusion is a Luxembourg non-profit founded in 2016, supported since 2022 by the Ministry of Family. Its three goals: make IT accessible to all, promote social inclusion through technology, and act for the environment by re-using and repairing donated equipment. Figures since 2016 (as published on the site, updated 03.03.2026): 10,507 computers and 1,466 smartphones distributed, 5,822 class participants. Free second-hand computers go to people living in Luxembourg who receive the cost-of-living allowance (allocation de vie chère), refugees (asylum seekers, beneficiaries of temporary protection, unaccompanied minors), holders of a "Vie Privée" residence permit, or persons in a collective debt-settlement procedure; current average waiting time under one month; since 22 September 2025 each household member aged 12+ may receive their own computer. Office: 1, Dernier Sol, Bonnevoie, L-2543 Luxembourg; open weekdays 9:30–17:30; walk-in "Open Classroom" every Friday 9:30–13:30. General information only — see digital-inclusion.lu for current details and conditions.*
