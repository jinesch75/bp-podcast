# Podcast Script — "CePAS – Psychological and School Support for Young People"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 14 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome back! It's a pleasure to have you with us today.
**TOM:** Hello everyone!
**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That is a programme about living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and cross-border families too, to know what support is out there.
**TOM:** And today we are talking about something close to many families' hearts... the wellbeing of young people, especially at school.
**ANNA:** School years can be wonderful. But they can also be stressful, confusing, and sometimes painful.
**TOM:** Worries about grades, about friendships, about the future, about what is happening at home.
**ANNA:** And that is exactly where today's organisation comes in. It is called CePAS.
**TOM:** CePAS. The full name is the Centre psycho-social et d'accompagnement scolaires.
**ANNA:** In simple terms... it is a psycho-social and school support centre for young people.
**TOM:** So Anna, what is the main idea behind CePAS?
**ANNA:** Its goal is to support the personal, relational, and academic development and wellbeing of young people at school.
**TOM:** So not only their marks, but the whole young person... their feelings, their relationships, their confidence.
**ANNA:** And it does this together with everyone around the young person... parents, teachers, and school staff.
**TOM:** I love that it has quite a history, too.
**ANNA:** It does. CePAS was born back in 1965, under an earlier name. So in 2025 it celebrated sixty years of supporting young people.
**TOM:** Sixty years. That's generations of young people who have been helped.
**ANNA:** Let's look at what CePAS actually offers. There are a few different parts.
**TOM:** The first, and the one families most often turn to, is its consultation centre for young people and families.
**ANNA:** This centre is for adolescents and young adults, roughly from twelve to thirty years old, and for their parents and families.
**TOM:** And what kind of help does it give?
**ANNA:** Psychological and therapeutic support, for school problems, personal problems, or relationship problems.
**TOM:** So if a young person is anxious, low, struggling with friends, or overwhelmed by school, they can find a professional to talk to.
**ANNA:** It also offers other forms of support, like psychomotor therapy, social support, and group activities.
**TOM:** These consultations are by appointment. And here is a recent, welcome change.
**ANNA:** Yes. Since early 2026, you can now have these consultations in English as well, not only in the country's other languages.
**TOM:** That opens the door for many more families in Luxembourg's international community.
**ANNA:** Now, there is a second part of the support, and it lives right inside the schools.
**TOM:** This is the school psycho-social service, present in every secondary school.
**ANNA:** So in each lycée, there are professionals a young person can talk to.
**TOM:** And an important detail... there, you often don't need an appointment.
**ANNA:** A young person can be seen quickly, sometimes the same day. That speed really matters in a crisis.
**TOM:** So think of it like two layers. Quick help inside the school, and deeper support at the consultation centre.
**ANNA:** Exactly. And both are there to catch a young person before a small worry becomes a big one.
**TOM:** There is also a third part, which works behind the scenes.
**ANNA:** Yes, a resource centre. It supports the staff who work in the schools' psycho-social services.
**TOM:** It helps them develop their skills and their professional practice.
**ANNA:** So CePAS does not only help young people directly. It also strengthens the whole network of people who support them.
**TOM:** Let's say something about cost and confidence, because families worry about both.
**ANNA:** The support is there to help, not to judge. It is a safe, professional, and caring space.
**TOM:** And a young person can speak openly, knowing they are being listened to with respect.
**ANNA:** CePAS also provides information on certain financial matters for pupils, when relevant.
**TOM:** Now Anna, this connects to another organisation we have talked about.
**ANNA:** It does. CePAS is also one of the partners that the Office National de l'Enfance can call on for psychological support.
**TOM:** So these services are not separate islands. They work together around the young person.
**ANNA:** That's the beauty of it. Many hands, one goal... a young person who feels supported.
**TOM:** Let's give a gentle, honest word about reaching out.
**ANNA:** If you are a young person listening, and things feel heavy, please know that asking for help is brave, not weak.
**TOM:** And if you are a parent, noticing your child is struggling, you are not failing. Support exists, and it works.
**ANNA:** Talking to someone early can change the path of a whole school year, and sometimes much more.
**TOM:** So Tom... let's make it practical. How does someone actually use CePAS?
**ANNA:** First... if your child needs quick support, the school's psycho-social service is right there, often without an appointment.
**TOM:** Second... for deeper or longer support, the consultation centre welcomes young people from twelve to thirty, and their families, by appointment.
**ANNA:** Third... remember that since 2026, consultations are also available in English.
**TOM:** And fourth... these services connect with others, like the ONE, so help can be coordinated.
**ANNA:** Let's bring it all together. What should people remember about CePAS?
**TOM:** First... it is a psycho-social and school support centre, helping young people's wellbeing at school for sixty years.
**ANNA:** Second... it offers psychological and therapeutic support for school, personal, and relationship problems.
**TOM:** Third... there is quick help inside every secondary school, and deeper support at its consultation centre.
**ANNA:** And fourth... it works hand in hand with parents, schools, and other services.
**TOM:** Young people carry more than we sometimes see. A listening ear at the right moment can mean everything.
**ANNA:** And in Luxembourg, that listening ear has a name... CePAS.
**TOM:** Thank you so much for listening today.
**ANNA:** And remember... if you or a young person you know is struggling, reaching out is always okay. Äddi!
**TOM:** Äddi!
