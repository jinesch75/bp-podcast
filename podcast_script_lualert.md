# Podcast Script — "LU-Alert: How Luxembourg Warns You in an Emergency"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 10 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today we talk about something that protects everybody. It's the system that warns the population when there is an emergency. It's called LU-Alert.

**ANNA:** LU-Alert. Okay, Tom, let's start at the very beginning. What does the name mean?

**TOM:** So, the name has two parts. "LU" is the international two-letter code for Luxembourg. And "Alert" is simply the English word for "alerte". So, LU-Alert... the alert for Luxembourg.

**ANNA:** Simple and clear. And what exactly is it?

**TOM:** LU-Alert is the national warning and information system for the population. It was deployed in autumn 2024. It's the modern replacement for the older warning system.

**ANNA:** And what is it for, really?

**TOM:** The goal is to warn and inform people who could be affected by a dangerous event. So you can prepare, and take the right precautions to protect yourself and your property. And it's not only during the emergency. It's also before — to build what they call resilience and prevention.

**ANNA:** So it helps me act at the right moment. Okay, the big question for most people... how does the warning actually reach me? On my phone?

**TOM:** Yes, mostly on your phone, and in several ways. LU-Alert is what we call "multi-channel". That means it uses many different channels at the same time, to reach as many people as possible.

**ANNA:** Many channels. Can you list them?

**TOM:** Of course. First, two ways that arrive directly on your mobile phone. One is Cell Broadcast. The other is location-based SMS.

**ANNA:** Let's take them one by one. What is Cell Broadcast?

**TOM:** Cell Broadcast sends a message to every compatible phone inside a chosen area. It uses a special, dedicated network. So it still works well even when the normal network is very busy.

**ANNA:** And location-based SMS?

**TOM:** That's a text message — an SMS — sent to the phones located in the targeted area. It works in a similar way, but it travels over the normal mobile network.

**ANNA:** Okay. And here's the question everybody asks. Do I need to sign up, or register, to get these?

**TOM:** No. And this is really important. For Cell Broadcast and for location-based SMS, you do not need to sign up. You don't give your number to anybody. If your phone is in the area when the authorities send a message, your phone is targeted automatically.

**ANNA:** So I don't have to do anything, and I can't really opt out either.

**TOM:** Correct. That's by design — so that everybody in danger gets warned.

**ANNA:** Good. You also mentioned other channels?

**TOM:** Yes. There is the LU-Alert mobile application, that you download for free. There are the official websites, like lu-alert dot l-u. There is the press — radio, television, their websites and apps. There is social media. And finally, sirens.

**ANNA:** Sirens? Like the old air-raid sirens?

**TOM:** Yes, but today the sirens are used only in one specific case — a nuclear alert. For everything else, it's the phone and the other channels.

**ANNA:** Got it. And the mobile app — what does it add, if I already get the SMS?

**TOM:** The app lets you follow all the alerts and information in one place, issued by the public authorities. You download it for free, from the Apple App Store or the Google Play Store. It's a good idea to install it.

**ANNA:** Now, a practical worry. Many of us don't speak Luxembourgish. In what language do these messages arrive?

**TOM:** Good question. The messages are sent in three languages — German, English and French. In the app, you receive the language you chose in the settings. By location-based SMS, you get all three languages in one message. So, English speakers are covered.

**ANNA:** That's reassuring. Okay, so a message arrives. What's inside it?

**TOM:** Normally, three things. First, what has happened, or what is likely to happen. Second, a recommendation — how you should behave, or what you should do. And third, a website where you can find more information.

**ANNA:** And if the message is very short?

**TOM:** That can happen, especially with SMS, because there is a character limit. So if the message is short, the advice is always the same — go to the website, lu-alert dot l-u, to read the full details of the alert.

**ANNA:** lu-alert.lu for the full story. Now, the messages also have levels, right? To show how serious it is?

**TOM:** Yes. LU-Alert uses different alert levels. Let me go through them. The strongest is "D", for imminent danger — that means take action immediately. Then there are three awareness levels, like the weather colours. Red is N1 — utmost vigilance, the highest. Orange is N2 — be careful, medium risk. Yellow is N3 — be aware, low risk.

**ANNA:** So red, orange, yellow — like a traffic light, almost.

**TOM:** Exactly, that's an easy way to remember it. And there are three more. "A" is for a missing person or a kidnapping alert. "I" is just information — no danger. And "T" is a test message.

**ANNA:** Ah, the test. So if I get a message that says it's a test...

**TOM:** ...then you don't need to do anything. The message will say clearly that it is a test. Luxembourg runs national tests — for example, there was one in February 2026. It's normal, don't worry.

**ANNA:** Very good to know. So nobody panics on a test day. Now — what kind of events can trigger an alert?

**TOM:** Quite a wide range. The system has twelve categories of events. Things like weather — storms, heavy rain, snow, cold, heat. The environment — for example floods, or air pollution. Health — like a food recall or an allergen warning. And then fire, rescue, security, safety, transport problems, failing infrastructure, and a few more.

**ANNA:** So from a big storm, to a flood, to a food recall.

**TOM:** Exactly. A wide range, all in one system. And these categories follow an international standard, so they fit with other countries too.

**ANNA:** And who actually sends these messages? Who decides?

**TOM:** Only public authorities. Several of them are connected to the system. For example — the Police, the Fire and Rescue Corps, which is the CGDIS, the weather service MeteoLux, the water management agency, the food administration, and the High Commission for National Protection.

**ANNA:** So it's always an official source. Never a private company.

**TOM:** Never. And one more thing — there is no advertising on LU-Alert. It is used only by public authorities, only for warning and information. Never for ads.

**ANNA:** That's a perfect link to my next worry. Scams. How do I know a message is real, and not phishing?

**TOM:** Very important topic. Like any message, an alert could be imitated by criminals. So, a few simple checks. First — the public authorities will never ask you for personal data. Never.

**ANNA:** Never share personal data. Okay.

**TOM:** Second — normally, the message will not contain a clickable link. If somehow there is a link, be careful, and check that it really points to an official government website. And third, the easiest check of all — go to lu-alert dot l-u and see if the same alert is listed there. If it's real, it will be on the website.

**ANNA:** So when in doubt, check the website. Don't click, don't share, just verify.

**TOM:** That's the golden rule.

**ANNA:** Now, two questions about people who might be left out. First — what if I don't have a smartphone?

**TOM:** Then you won't get the app messages, or Cell Broadcast. But you can still receive a location-based SMS on a normal phone. And you always have the other channels — the website, other government websites, the radio and television.

**ANNA:** And someone with a visual impairment?

**TOM:** Most phones have accessibility settings. You can let the phone read the message out loud, or make the text bigger. So the message can be heard, not only seen.

**ANNA:** That's thoughtful. Okay, Tom — let's make it really practical. If I'm listening now, what should I do today, to be ready?

**TOM:** Three simple things. One — download the LU-Alert app, from your app store, and choose your language. Two — check that your phone can receive these alerts; you can look in your phone settings, under emergency or government alerts. And three — remember the website, lu-alert dot l-u, and remember the golden rule: verify there, never share personal data.

**ANNA:** Download the app, check your settings, remember the website. Easy. Okay, short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. LU-Alert is Luxembourg's national warning and information system, launched in autumn 2024. It reaches you on your phone — by Cell Broadcast and location-based SMS, with no sign-up needed — and also through the app, websites, the media and, for nuclear alerts, sirens. Messages come in German, English and French, with a level from yellow up to imminent danger, and they tell you what happened and what to do.

**ANNA:** And the safety message?

**TOM:** The authorities never ask for personal data, and there are no ads. If you are not sure, check lu-alert dot l-u.

**ANNA:** Beautiful. So... an emergency is rare, but being ready costs you nothing. Download the app today, and you're prepared.

**TOM:** Really. A few minutes now... real peace of mind later.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about LU-Alert, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and staying safe together. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!
