# Podcast Script — "Info-Seniors: Finding the Right Help for Older People"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 10 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today's topic touches almost every family, sooner or later. It's about growing older — and about a service that helps you find the right support. It's called Info-Seniors.

**ANNA:** Info-Seniors. Okay, Tom, let's start simply. What is it?

**TOM:** So, Info-Seniors is the official information service for older people in Luxembourg. Its home on the internet is a website — infosenior dot l-u.

**ANNA:** infosenior.lu. And who is behind it?

**TOM:** It's run by the State — by the Ministry of Family. So it's an official, public source. Not a private company trying to sell you something.

**ANNA:** Good to know. And who is it for? Only for older people themselves?

**TOM:** No, and this is important. It's for seniors, yes — but also for their families, for the professionals who work with them, and really, for anyone interested. So if you are worried about an ageing parent, or a neighbour, this is for you too.

**ANNA:** That's reassuring. Because often it's the children, or the family, who go looking for help.

**TOM:** Exactly. Especially for cross-border workers — maybe your parents live in another country, and you want to understand what exists here. Or the opposite — you work here, and you're arranging help for a parent in Luxembourg.

**ANNA:** So, what do you actually find on the website? What's the heart of it?

**TOM:** The heart of it is something called the Public Register of Services for Seniors.

**ANNA:** A register. That sounds official. What's inside?

**TOM:** It's a complete, reliable list of the services and structures for older people in Luxembourg. For each one, you find detailed information — what the service is, the conditions to use it, and the steps you have to take.

**ANNA:** So not just a name and a phone number. It tells me how to actually get the help.

**TOM:** Exactly. What it is, who can use it, and how to start. That's the useful part.

**ANNA:** Now you said "register" and "official". Is there something special about which services are listed?

**TOM:** Yes — and this is really the key point. The register only lists services that are approved by the State. In French, we say they have an "agrément".

**ANNA:** An agrément. So, an official authorisation.

**TOM:** Right. In Luxembourg, anyone who offers care, accommodation, help or guidance to older people must have this approval from the Ministry. To get it, they have to meet quality requirements. And the Ministry carries out regular checks.

**ANNA:** So if a service is in the register...

**TOM:** ...it has been checked and approved by the State. That's a real guarantee of quality. You're not picking a name at random from the internet.

**ANNA:** That's a big deal, especially for something as sensitive as caring for a parent. And how recent is this register?

**TOM:** It's built on a law that's in force since the first of March, 2024. And it's updated regularly, so the information stays reliable and up to date.

**ANNA:** Perfect. Okay, Tom — let's get concrete. What kind of services can I find there?

**TOM:** A whole range, for every level of need. Let me go through the main ones. First — care homes, the residential structures, for people who can no longer live at home.

**ANNA:** Okay, the classic retirement or care home.

**TOM:** Yes. Then — assisted living, where you have your own home but with support and security nearby. Then — day care centres, where a senior can spend the day, with activities and company, and go home in the evening.

**ANNA:** Ah, that's nice for someone who's still at home but shouldn't be alone all day.

**TOM:** Exactly. And it also gives the family a break. Then there's help at home — household help, and nursing care that comes to you. There's "meals on wheels" — warm meals delivered to your door.

**ANNA:** Meals on wheels. That's a lovely, practical one.

**TOM:** And there's telecare — sometimes called a telealarm. It's a little device, often a button you wear, that lets you call for help immediately if you fall or feel unwell.

**ANNA:** So you can stay at home, but with a safety net. That gives real peace of mind.

**TOM:** That's the idea. The goal of all of this — and it's the goal of the whole national policy — is to let people stay independent, at home, for as long as possible.

**ANNA:** And you mentioned activities too?

**TOM:** Yes. Beyond care, there are senior activities — for staying active, learning, and meeting people. For example, there are the Clubs Aktiv Plus, the senior clubs all around the country, where older people meet, do activities, and stay connected.

**ANNA:** Because staying active and social is part of staying healthy.

**TOM:** Completely. Loneliness is a real risk as we age. And that's one of the things the system tries hard to prevent.

**ANNA:** Speaking of which — are there services for more specific situations? Not everyone has the same needs.

**TOM:** Yes, and the website points you to specialised services too. Let me give a few examples. For memory problems and dementia, there's a national information centre — the Info-Zenter Demenz. And there's the Alzheimer association, which even runs a help line, available around the clock, for urgent situations.

**ANNA:** A 24-hour line for Alzheimer — that's so important for families in distress.

**TOM:** It really is. Then, for the digital world — there's an organisation that helps seniors learn to use computers and smartphones, so they're not left behind. For loneliness, there's a service of the Red Cross, where trained volunteers regularly visit isolated people.

**ANNA:** Just to keep them company. That's beautiful.

**TOM:** It is. And for very difficult moments — there's palliative care and end-of-life support, and there's information about your rights and wishes for the end of life. So the system covers the whole journey, with respect.

**ANNA:** That's a caring approach. And is there a place that works on the knowledge itself — research, training for the people who care?

**TOM:** Yes. There's a competence centre for ageing that has existed for about thirty years. It does three things — activities and learning for seniors, continuing education for care professionals, and research on ageing. So the quality keeps improving over time.

**ANNA:** So it's not standing still. Good. Tom, let's make this very real. Can we take an example?

**TOM:** Of course. Let's imagine your mother is eighty, she lives alone, and she's still quite independent — but you're starting to worry a little.

**ANNA:** A very common situation. So what does Info-Seniors do for me?

**TOM:** You go to the website, and you look at what fits. Maybe, first, just meals on wheels, so she eats well every day. Maybe a telecare button, for safety, in case she falls. Maybe a Club Aktiv Plus nearby, so she sees people every week.

**ANNA:** So I can build a kind of package — a little help here, a little there — to keep her safely at home.

**TOM:** Exactly. And later, if she needs more, you already know where to look — day care a few days a week, then more home nursing, and only much later, if needed, a care home. Step by step, at her pace.

**ANNA:** And for each of those, the register tells me the conditions and the steps.

**TOM:** For each one. So instead of panicking, you have a calm, clear map.

**ANNA:** I really like that — a calm, clear map. And if my mother doesn't speak much, or is afraid of technology?

**TOM:** Then the digital-skills service can help her learn, gently. Or a volunteer can visit. The point is, there's almost always something — you just have to know it exists. And that's exactly what the portal is for — so you know it exists.

**ANNA:** Now, Tom — a practical worry. What if I have a problem with a service? Or I just don't know who to ask?

**TOM:** Good question. There is a national service for information and mediation in this field. So if you need to be guided, or if there's a disagreement with a service, there's a neutral place that can help you find a solution.

**ANNA:** So you're never completely on your own with it.

**TOM:** Never. Between the website, the register, and that information and mediation service, there's always a door to knock on.

**ANNA:** Let's zoom out for a second. You said this is part of a bigger policy. What's the big idea?

**TOM:** The big idea is what they call "ageing well". The State wants older people to live as independently as possible, for as long as possible — and to stay part of society, not pushed to the side. So the work covers many things — preparing for retirement, lifelong learning, volunteering, fighting isolation, the digital world, and connecting the generations.

**ANNA:** Connecting the generations — young and old together.

**TOM:** Yes. Ageing isn't only about care. It's also about staying active, useful, and connected. And there's also financial support — for people who don't have enough money to cover the cost of care, the State helps.

**ANNA:** That's important — that money shouldn't be the barrier to getting care. Okay, Tom, let's make it really practical. If I'm listening, and I — or my parent — might need some help, what do I do?

**TOM:** Very simple. Go to the website — infosenior dot l-u. Look through the Public Register to find approved services — a care home, day care, help at home, meals, telecare — whatever fits the need. For each one, you'll see what it is, the conditions, and the steps to take. And if you're not sure, use the information and mediation service to be guided.

**ANNA:** And because it's official, I can trust what I find there.

**TOM:** Exactly. Official, free, and quality-checked. That's the whole point.

**ANNA:** Short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. Info-Seniors, on infosenior dot l-u, is the State's information portal for older people in Luxembourg. Its heart is the Public Register of Services for Seniors — a reliable, regularly updated list of services approved and checked by the Ministry. You'll find care homes, assisted living, day care, home help and nursing, meals on wheels, telecare, and senior activities — with the conditions and the steps for each.

**ANNA:** And the message for today?

**TOM:** When it comes to growing older — yours, or a loved one's — you don't have to search blindly. There's one official, trustworthy place to start, and there's help to guide you.

**ANNA:** So... whether it's for yourself, your parents, or a neighbour — remember the name. Info-Seniors. infosenior.lu.

**TOM:** Really. One website, and a whole network of support behind it.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about Info-Seniors, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and looking out for each other. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!
