# Podcast Script — "The National Solidarity Fund: Luxembourg's Safety Net"

**Part of the Biergerpakt programme**
**Hosts:** Anna (woman) and Tom (man)
**Length:** about 15 minutes — spoken slowly, in simple English

---

**ANNA:** Hello, and welcome! It's really nice to have you with us today.

**TOM:** Hello everyone!

**ANNA:** This episode is part of the Biergerpakt. That's a programme of living together in Luxembourg. The idea is simple... we want to help residents, and also cross-border workers, to discover Luxembourg — or re-discover it — and to meet new people.

**TOM:** Exactly. And today's topic is, I think, one of the most important of all. It's about solidarity — about the help that's there when life gets hard.

**ANNA:** And there's one office at the heart of that. Tom, what is it called?

**TOM:** It's called the National Solidarity Fund. In French, the Fonds national de solidarité. People often just say the short name — F-N-S.

**ANNA:** The National Solidarity Fund. That's a beautiful name. Let's start simply. What is it?

**TOM:** The FNS is a public office whose whole job is to help people who are struggling financially. It's run under the Ministry of Family. And its mission, very simply, is the fight against poverty.

**ANNA:** So this is the country's safety net.

**TOM:** That's exactly the right word. A safety net. When someone's income is too low to live with dignity — because of a job loss, an illness, old age, a separation, or simply low pay — the FNS is there. Its home on the internet is f-n-s dot l-u.

**ANNA:** fns.lu. And it's official — the State.

**TOM:** Completely official. Not a charity, not a private fund. It's the State, organising solidarity for everyone who needs it.

**ANNA:** Okay. So who is it for? Who can knock on this door?

**TOM:** Mostly people who live in Luxembourg and whose income is modest, or who are in a difficult situation. Now — the FNS doesn't do just one thing. It manages a whole set of different helps. Let me walk through the main ones.

**ANNA:** Yes, let's go through them one by one. Where do we start?

**TOM:** Let's start with the biggest one. It's called the social inclusion income — in French, the revenu d'inclusion sociale. The short name is REVIS.

**ANNA:** REVIS. What is it?

**TOM:** REVIS is the guaranteed minimum income. The idea is simple but powerful — in Luxembourg, nobody should fall below a certain basic level. If your household income is under that level, REVIS tops you up to it.

**ANNA:** So it makes sure you always have a minimum to live on.

**TOM:** Exactly. A floor, below which the State doesn't let you fall. The amount depends on your household — how many adults, how many children. There's a base amount for the first adult, more for a second adult, and an amount for each child.

**ANNA:** So a single person gets one level, and a bigger family gets more, because they need more.

**TOM:** Precisely. It's calculated for the whole household, and it counts the income you already have. REVIS fills the gap between what you have and the minimum you're entitled to.

**ANNA:** And is it only money? Or is there more to it?

**TOM:** That's the clever part of REVIS. It's not only a cheque. The word "inclusion" is important. There's also a side that helps people get back on their feet — activation, they call it. Support to find work, training, or useful activity, so the help is a bridge, not a dead end.

**ANNA:** I like that — money to live on, and a hand to move forward.

**TOM:** That's the whole philosophy. And if someone takes part in an activation measure — like community work as part of their plan — there can be an activation allowance on top.

**ANNA:** Good. So that's REVIS — the minimum income. What's the second big help?

**TOM:** The second is something many more people can get — the cost-of-living allowance. In French, the allocation de vie chère.

**ANNA:** The cost-of-living allowance. Tell me about it.

**TOM:** It's a yearly payment to help modest-income households with the rising cost of everyday life. And here's the key — you don't have to be on REVIS to get it. You can be working, on a small or medium salary, and still qualify, as long as your income is under a certain limit.

**ANNA:** Ah, so this reaches a lot of ordinary households — people who work, but for whom every euro counts.

**TOM:** Exactly. And the amount depends on the size of your household. A single person gets one amount, a couple more, a family more again. Right now it's roughly between one thousand eight hundred euros for a single person, up to around three thousand six hundred for a large household, each year.

**ANNA:** That's a real, meaningful sum. And you said there's more attached to it?

**TOM:** Yes — and this is important with today's energy prices. Together with the cost-of-living allowance, there's an energy premium — extra help with heating and electricity bills. And for older people on a small income, there can be an extra financial aid for seniors as well.

**ANNA:** So one application can open three doors — cost-of-living, energy, and senior aid.

**TOM:** One application, yes. And here's a wonderful detail — if you already receive REVIS, you don't even have to apply. The cost-of-living allowance, the energy premium, and the senior aid come to you automatically.

**ANNA:** That's thoughtful — the people who need it most don't have to fill in another form.

**TOM:** Right. For everyone else, you apply each year. For the year twenty twenty-six, for example, you can apply across the whole year, up to the end of December. And the FNS even has an online calculator, so you can check if you qualify before you apply.

**ANNA:** A calculator — that's really helpful, so you're not applying blindly. Okay Tom — what's next on the list?

**TOM:** The third help is for people living with a serious disability. It's the income for severely disabled persons — in French, the revenu pour personnes gravement handicapées.

**ANNA:** So a dedicated income for people who can't work because of a serious disability.

**TOM:** Exactly. For a person whose disability means they can't earn a living in the ordinary job market, the FNS provides a guaranteed income, so they too have a secure floor to stand on.

**ANNA:** That's solidarity in the truest sense. What comes next?

**TOM:** The fourth one is very different, and very practical. It's about maintenance payments — what a parent is supposed to pay for their child after a separation. In French, the pension alimentaire.

**ANNA:** Ah — child support. And what happens when it isn't paid?

**TOM:** That's exactly the problem the FNS solves. Imagine a separated parent — often a mother — who is owed child support, but the other parent simply doesn't pay. The child suffers. So the FNS can step in, and advance that money.

**ANNA:** Wait — so the State pays the support that the other parent didn't?

**TOM:** Within certain limits and conditions, yes. The FNS advances the unpaid maintenance, so the child and the parent aren't left with nothing. And then — this is the clever part — the FNS goes after the parent who didn't pay, to recover the money.

**ANNA:** So the State acts as a kind of guarantor, and then does the chasing itself.

**TOM:** Exactly. The vulnerable parent doesn't have to fight alone. The FNS takes on that burden. It's one of the most human things the Fund does.

**ANNA:** That really is. Okay — how many more?

**TOM:** Two more to mention. The fifth is a supplement for older people who need care. When someone goes into a care home, or assisted living, and their own income isn't enough to cover the cost, the FNS can help pay the difference.

**ANNA:** So an older person isn't turned away from care just because their pension is too small.

**TOM:** That's the idea. Care according to need, not only according to your bank balance. The Fund tops up what's missing, so dignity in old age is protected.

**ANNA:** And the sixth?

**TOM:** The sixth is the child-raising pension. In Luxembourgish, people call it the "Mammerent". It's a small monthly amount for parents — historically mothers — who spent years raising children and so built up less pension of their own.

**ANNA:** Ah — so it recognises the years given to raising a family.

**TOM:** Exactly. It's a way of saying — that work mattered too. It's a modest amount each month, but it honours those years.

**ANNA:** So let's gather them up. We have REVIS, the minimum income. The cost-of-living allowance, with energy premium and senior aid. The income for severely disabled persons. The advance on maintenance payments. The supplement for older people in care. And the child-raising pension, the Mammerent.

**TOM:** Six helps, one office. And behind them all, one single idea — that in Luxembourg, no one should be left to fall.

**ANNA:** Tom, let me ask the practical question. How does someone actually apply? It sounds like it could be complicated.

**TOM:** It's more approachable than people fear. Everything starts at the website, f-n-s dot l-u. There you find each help explained, with the conditions, and the forms.

**ANNA:** And can you do it online?

**TOM:** For several things, yes — through MyGuichet.lu, the State's digital platform, with a secure login. And for the cost-of-living allowance, there's that online calculator first. But you can also do it on paper — download the form, or get it by post, fill it in, and send it back to the FNS.

**ANNA:** And if someone is confused, or doesn't know which help is for them?

**TOM:** Then they can contact the FNS directly — by phone, by email, or in person. The offices are on the rue de la Fonderie, in Luxembourg City. And honestly, that first phone call is often the most important step. Because many people don't claim what they're entitled to — simply because they don't know it exists.

**ANNA:** That's such an important point. Help that you don't know about doesn't help you.

**TOM:** That's the whole reason we're doing this episode. There is money and support waiting — but you have to reach for it.

**ANNA:** Let's make it real, Tom. Can we follow one person?

**TOM:** Of course. Let's imagine a woman — let's call her Sofia. She's recently separated, she has two young children, and she works part-time on a small salary.

**ANNA:** A very real situation. Where does the FNS come in for Sofia?

**TOM:** In several places at once. First — her income is low, so she may qualify for the cost-of-living allowance, with the energy premium, to help through the year.

**ANNA:** That's the yearly boost.

**TOM:** Yes. Second — her ex-partner is supposed to pay child support, but he's stopped. So the FNS can advance that maintenance, and chase him for it — so her children don't go without.

**ANNA:** That takes a huge weight off her shoulders.

**TOM:** A huge weight. And third — if even with her salary the household is still below the minimum, REVIS can top her up to that floor — and at the same time, help her with training or finding more work.

**ANNA:** So from one office, Sofia gets a yearly allowance, her children's support secured, and a guaranteed minimum with a path forward.

**TOM:** All from one place. That's the power of having a single solidarity fund. It can wrap several supports around the same family.

**ANNA:** And the key, again, is that she has to know to ask.

**TOM:** That's the key. Which is why, if you take one thing from today — it's this. If money is tight, don't suffer in silence. Check the FNS. You may be entitled to far more than you think.

**ANNA:** Okay Tom, let's bring it home. If I'm listening, and things are difficult, what do I do?

**TOM:** Three simple steps. First — go to the website, f-n-s dot l-u. Look at the helps, and see which ones fit your situation.

**ANNA:** fns.lu. Got it.

**TOM:** Second — if your income is modest, use the cost-of-living calculator, and apply. And if you're on REVIS already, remember — that one comes automatically.

**ANNA:** And third?

**TOM:** Third — if you're not sure, just contact them. A phone call, an email, a visit. Ask the question. There's no shame in solidarity — that's literally what the Fund is for.

**ANNA:** A short summary to finish?

**TOM:** Sure. The National Solidarity Fund — the FNS, fns.lu — is Luxembourg's safety net against poverty. It manages REVIS, the guaranteed minimum income; the cost-of-living allowance with the energy premium and senior aid; an income for severely disabled persons; advances on unpaid child maintenance; a supplement for older people in care; and the child-raising pension, the Mammerent. You apply on fns.lu or MyGuichet.lu — and REVIS recipients get the cost-of-living allowance automatically.

**ANNA:** And the message for today?

**TOM:** Solidarity in Luxembourg isn't just a word — it's an office, with a budget, and a phone number. If life gets hard, that net is real, and it's there for you. You only have to reach for it.

**ANNA:** So... if you, or someone you know, is struggling — remember the name. The National Solidarity Fund. fns.lu.

**TOM:** One fund, holding up everyone who needs it. That's what solidarity means.

**ANNA:** That was our episode about the National Solidarity Fund, part of the Biergerpakt — living together in Luxembourg, discovering the country, and looking out for each other. Thank you so much for listening.

**TOM:** Thank you, everyone. Take care... and see you next time!

**ANNA:** Bye bye!

*Sources: fns.public.lu (Fonds national de solidarité — National Solidarity Fund) and guichet.public.lu official pages, including the FNS body page and "Social parameters" (values from 1 January 2026). The FNS, under the Ministry of Family Affairs, Solidarity, Living Together and Reception of Refugees, manages: the social inclusion income (REVIS); the allowance for severely disabled persons (RPGH); the cost-of-living benefit, energy allowance and financial assistance for seniors (cost-of-living benefit roughly €1,817 for a single person up to €3,637 for a household of 5+ per year; energy allowance €600–€1,200); advance on and recovery of maintenance payments; the supplement for seniors in care; and the child-raising pension ('Mammerent'). REVIS recipients receive the cost-of-living allowance, energy premium and senior aid automatically. Amounts are indexed and change over time — check the official pages on fns.lu and guichet.lu for current figures and conditions. General information only.*
